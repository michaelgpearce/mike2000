JUNKSHOP GLAM FROM THE WRONG ADDRESS, 1968–1979

Glam Without a Map

April 3, 2026

The bands in Nobody’s Charts at least showed up at the right time, in the right country, with platform boots on. These ones missed a connecting flight. A Dutch band in 1973 doing it better than half of Carnaby Street. A future British glam band in 1968 who hadn’t figured that out yet. A CBS B-side from 1975 that was better than the A-side and sold nothing. An American cover of a Sweet single, recorded in Los Angeles, produced by Michael Lloyd. Glam has borders — all of these records crossed them without noticing.

  1. 01

    The Mud — "Up The Airy Mountain"

    1968 · CBS · UK

    Not Mud — the Mud. On CBS in 1968, five years before “Tiger Feet” and “Lonely This Christmas” and all those chart-topping glam-pop runs. The same band, not yet aware of what they were going to become, recording a slightly psych-tinged single with a title lifted from a William Allingham poem about faeries. It flopped. They kept going, shed the “the,” shed the psychedelia, and built one of the stranger careers in British pop. The proto-glam you didn’t know to listen for.

  2. 02

    Porky — "I’m An Artist"

    1971 · Bell Records · UK/US

    Shel Talmy — the American who produced the Who and the Kinks and gave British rock its first proper crunch — revisited a theme he’d explored with the Creation in 1966 and came back with this: a tight little glam-before-it-was-glam single by a band called Porky that nobody has ever been able to fully identify. Presumably British, released only in the US on Bell, gone immediately. The diminished chord under the chorus is a very Talmy thing to do. The rest of it just drives.

  3. 03

    Zingara — "Girl Girl Girl"

    1973 · Pink Elephant · Netherlands

    Coevorden is a small Dutch town near the German border, and it produced Zingara, who in 1973 recorded a glam cruncher on Pink Elephant that holds up against anything the British second division was releasing. The stomp is correct. The riff is correct. The whole thing is two minutes and forty-five seconds of heavy glam boogie that didn’t need a Carnaby Street address. The signal reached the Netherlands and somebody in Coevorden answered it properly.

  4. 04

    Terry Williams — "Wig Wam Bam"

    1973 · MGM · USA

    The lead guitarist from Kenny Rogers and the First Edition, late of the New Christy Minstrels, in the sunken-living-room clutches of producer Michael Lloyd, covering a year-old Sweet hit for the hot-panted crowds at Rodney’s English Disco. That sentence should not produce a record this good. It does. Lloyd brought in guitars that sound like the Flamin’ Groovies, Williams delivered it with complete conviction, and the result is the best American glam single that nobody counts as American glam. It didn’t chart. Williams went back to Kenny Rogers. Nobody can explain it.

  5. 05

    Geoff Whitehorn — "U.F.O."

    1974 · Stateside · UK

    He went on to play with Roger Chapman, Back Street Crawler, and eventually Procol Harum. In 1974 he was the guitarist in If — the jazz-rock group — and released a solo album on Stateside that contains this: a straight-ahead glam stomp so confident it sounds like a hit from someone who made hits. He did not make hits. The album contained jazz, rock, and songs about UFOs. Nobody was ready for it to be all three things at once. The guitar playing is exceptional throughout.

  6. 06

    Merlin — "Rock And Roll Express"

    1975 · CBS · UK

    Merlin were a London band who made a schizoid album that alternated hard rock with pop glam and prog and couldn’t settle on any of them. Their fourth single was “What A Funny Way To Fall In Love” b/w “Rock And Roll Express.” The A-side is forgettable. The B-side is a killer — a flat-out glam banger that was exclusive to the flip and went nowhere while it was there. Someone in the band knew what they were doing with this one. They buried it anyway.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  7. 07

    Scarface — "Dance To The Band"

    1975 · DJM Records · UK

    DJM was Dick James’s label — Elton John built his career there, then famously had to fight to get his publishing back. In 1975 it was also home to Scarface, who made this: mid-tempo glam with a chorus that sounds like it should have been bigger than it was, which is a description that fits most of the records on this list. Sweet had already peaked. Slade was pivoting. The formula still worked. Scarface used it correctly and sold nothing. DJS 616 is the catalog number. There was one more single and then silence.

  8. 08

    Giggles — "Just Another Saturday Night"

    1976 · EMI · UK

    A cover of a track from Canadian band Moonquake’s second album, released in the UK on EMI in 1976 by a band called Giggles, who nobody in Canada had heard of and nobody in the UK paid much attention to either. The British version beats the original: it’s a glam-boogie crossover with enough production texture to reward replay. Giggles had four singles on EMI before becoming the Giants on RCA, which was a different band name attached to the same absence of commercial success. The sleeve, apparently, was very colorful.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  9. 09

    Fringe Benefit — "All In Vain"

    1977 · Polydor · UK

    Glam was dead by 1977. Fringe Benefit from Poole in Dorset had not received this information. “All In Vain” is the lead track from their self-titled album on Capricorn in the US and Polydor in the UK, and it is a completely confident glam rock song released into a world that had moved on to punk and didn’t want it. They released three singles and played very rarely. John Jones went on to form QED, who are still playing the Dorset circuit. The record sounds like it came out in 1974. It did not come out in 1974.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  10. 10

    Philadelphia — "School’s Back"

    1977 · UK

    Named after the city, operating nowhere near it. “School’s Back” arrived in 1977 — the same year as “Anarchy in the U.K.,” the same year the rules changed — and sounded like a glam single from three years earlier that had gotten lost in the post. By this point putting platforms on was an act of willful defiance. Philadelphia were defiant. It didn’t help. The record has all the right moves and absolutely none of the timing. Which makes it exactly right for this list.
