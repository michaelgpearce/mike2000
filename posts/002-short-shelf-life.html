WEEK 2 — LATE 70S PUNKY POWER POP

Short Shelf Life

Ten songs from 1976–1980 that came out fast, sold nothing, and were right about everything. The punk energy got grafted onto bubblegum hooks and somewhere in there was a perfect record that nobody heard. Some of these you know. Most you don't. All of them deserve better.

  1. 01

    Fast Cars — "The Kids Just Wanna Dance"

    1979 · Rabid Records · Manchester, UK

    One 45. That's all they left. Manchester 1979, Rabid Records, and then nothing. No album, no follow-up, just this perfect thing that sounds like the entire argument for power pop made in under three minutes. The title is not subtle and the song doesn't need to be. Catchy like a punch.

  2. 02

    The Nerves — "Hanging on the Telephone"

    1976 · Nerves Records · Los Angeles, CA

    Yes, Blondie covered this. Yes, it's on Spotify. But if you've only heard the Blondie version you're missing the point — the Nerves recorded it scrappier, faster, and with a vocal performance that sounds like someone actually desperate. Peter Case, Jack Lee, Paul Collins. All three went on to make essential records. This was first.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  3. 03

    Rubber City Rebels — "Childeaters"

    1978 · Clone Records · Akron, OH

    Akron was briefly one of the strangest places in American music — Devo, Pere Ubu, and tucked in there, the Rubber City Rebels doing something that sounds like the Stooges tried to write a hit. Nasty guitar, stupid-catchy, deeply weird. Clone Records out of Cleveland, which tells you everything you need to know about the distribution situation.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  4. 04

    The Incredible Kidda Band — "Everybody Knows"

    1981 · Last Laugh Records · Newcastle, UK

    Newcastle, which is the last place you'd go looking for this sound. Four kids who clearly owned a copy of every Raspberries album and saw no reason to pretend otherwise. "Everybody Knows" is the one that got reissued years later because people kept asking about it. The answer was always: some kids from Newcastle, in 1981, who got it exactly right once.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  5. 05

    Pointed Sticks — "I'm Numb"

    1979 · Quintessence Records · Vancouver, BC

    Vancouver 1979, which had its own small explosion happening — DOA on one end, the Pointed Sticks on the other. They were the power pop band in a scene that mostly wanted to get heavier. "I'm Numb" has got that specific feeling of a song written at 3am and recorded the next afternoon because the studio time was already booked.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  6. 06

    The Modernettes — "Teen City"

    1980 · Mo-Da-Mu Records · Vancouver, BC

    Same Vancouver scene. Buck Cherry on guitar (not that one — the original one, John Armstrong), and a bassist who went by Mary Jo Kopechne, which is either very funny or extremely dark depending on your age. "Teen City" is the lead track off their EP and it sounds like what would happen if the Buzzcocks decided hooks mattered more than misery. They were right.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  7. 07

    The Gooses — "Just a Tailor"

    1977 · Private Press

    Nobody knows much about the Gooses. That's the whole appeal. Private press 1977, sounds like it came out of somebody's garage in a suburb that no longer exists. "Just a Tailor" has got this late bubblegum energy that went slightly sideways — the melody is too good for the production and that's exactly what makes it work. Filed under: found in a box at an estate sale.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  8. 08

    The Atlantics — "Television Girl"

    1979 · MCA · Boston, MA

    Boston had a whole scene in 1979 that never really got its due — the Outlets, the Atlantics, a bunch of bands playing the same circuit that should have broken nationally and didn't. "Television Girl" is the kind of song that sounds like it charted somewhere and you just missed it. It didn't. It got maybe three radio spins and then the album sat in a warehouse.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  9. 09

    The Outlets — "Knock Me Down"

    1980 · Boys Life / Outlets split EP · Boston, MA

    Same Boston circuit, different band. The Outlets came out on a split EP with Boys Life, produced by Richie Parsons of Unnatural Axe, and "Knock Me Down" became their regional calling card. Regional meaning: people in Boston knew it, everybody else didn't. Rick Barton later joined the Dropkick Murphys. The Outlets never got the credit they deserved.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  10. 10

    The Shake Shakes — "You Can Run"

    1979 · Notown Records

    Private press, Notown Records, 1979. They pressed it, nobody bought it, they released a second single, and then vanished. Years later someone dug it up and posted it and people lost their minds a little. The bassist, Richard Snyder, later played with Captain Beefheart's band, which contextualizes absolutely nothing about this song, but is a good fact. "You Can Run" is an unsung power pop classic and the internet has largely agreed on this even if it came about 40 years too late to matter.
