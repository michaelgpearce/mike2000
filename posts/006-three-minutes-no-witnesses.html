PURE POWER POP 1976–1991

Three Minutes, No Witnesses

March 9, 2026

Ten songs built to the same exact specification: maximum hooks, minimum running time, zero commercial traction. The Big Star lineage in full — Memphis to Melbourne, Chicago to Zion, Illinois. Every song here could have been a hit. Most of them should have been. Welcome to the neighborhood.

  1. 01

    Flamin’ Groovies — “Shake Some Action”

    1976 · Sire Records · San Francisco, CA

    They recorded it in London with Dave Edmunds, who understood exactly what they were going for — a song with Buddy Holly DNA, Stones swagger, and somehow sounded completely new in 1976. Cyril Jordan had been dragging the Groovies toward this through two different lineups and a string of labels; this was the arrival. It didn’t chart. Of course.

  2. 02

    The Scruffs — “My Mind”

    1977 · Power Play Records · Memphis, TN

    Big Star’s city, Big Star’s influence — except the Scruffs were doing it scrappier and cheaper. Ardent Studios shows up in the credits here too, which tells you everything about the Memphis ecosystem. “My Mind” is the kind of song that sounds obvious and inevitable once you’ve heard it. Then you go back and listen three more times because you want to know how they did it.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  3. 03

    Pezband — “Come On Madeline”

    1978 · Passport Records · Chicago, IL

    Chicago power pop with distribution luck so bad the album was better known in Britain than in their home city. Passport got Laughing in the Dark to UK stores before US stores, which is a sentence that explains nothing about why this didn’t connect but explains everything about the era. “Come On Madeline” is the track with the hook that functions like load-bearing walls.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  4. 04

    The Rubinoos — “I Wanna Be Your Boyfriend”

    1978 · Beserkley Records · Berkeley, CA

    Beserkley was the label for this in the mid-70s Bay Area — also home to Jonathan Richman and Greg Kihn. Pure bubblegum refracted through punk energy: fast, wide-eyed, catastrophically catchy. Got UK airplay before US airplay, which tells you something. Avril Lavigne later borrowed heavily enough from this that they settled out of court. The Rubinoos were right about everything.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  5. 05

    The Sports — “Reckless”

    1978 · Mushroom Records · Melbourne, Australia

    Melbourne power pop with Stephen Cummings, who sounds like he was born in the wrong country for his talents. Australia had an underground that tracked UK and US punk and power pop in real time, and the Sports were making the most melodic sense of it. “Reckless” is the title track off their debut — which is a thing that can happen when your debut album is this confident.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  6. 06

    The Jags — “Back of My Hand”

    1979 · Island Records · Scarborough, UK

    This one actually hit #17 in the UK. Consider it the control group. The Jags were peak Costello-adjacent power pop — chiming guitars, nervous energy, perfect construction. Got a Top of the Pops appearance. And then nothing. The follow-up album didn’t connect, the moment passed. Even the songs that made it still didn’t really make it.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  7. 07

    Shoes — “Burned Out Love”

    1981 · PVC Records · Zion, IL

    Zion, Illinois. Population 24,000. Home to one of the best power pop bands America ever produced. The Bonn brothers self-recorded their early stuff in a basement, which is insane given how polished it sounds. By 1981’s Tongue Twister they had a deal but none of the commercial follow-through. “Burned Out Love” is the wistful side of Shoes — strings instead of jangle, regret instead of velocity, still better than most of what was on the radio.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  8. 08

    The Plimsouls — “Play the Breaks”

    1983 · Frontier Records · Los Angeles, CA

    Peter Case’s band had the look and the sound and even a moment — the Valley Girl soundtrack put “A Million Miles Away” in front of an actual audience. “Play the Breaks” is the Plimsouls at their most muscular, from their final album before Case went solo. Good song that came too late in the band’s run to do anything. Case went on to make essential records. The Plimsouls mostly went unmourned, which is wrong.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  9. 09

    Tommy Keene — “Places That Are Gone”

    1986 · Geffen Records · Washington, DC

    Geffen signed Keene. Geffen had no idea what to do with Keene. This song is maybe the platonic ideal of what power pop sounds like when it grows up a little — the hooks are all there but there’s something elegiac underneath, a melody that sounds like it’s trying to hold onto something. Keene kept making records and touring for thirty years and died in 2017. Still the most underrated person in the genre.

    ▶ Watch on YouTube
  10. 10

    Material Issue — “Valerie Loves Me”

    1991 · Island Records · Chicago, IL

    College radio, a real label, a real video — and it still didn’t break. Jim Ellison had everything figured out: the dynamics, the production, the economy of a great pop song. The band peaked at “college radio favorite” and nowhere near there. International Pop Overthrow is a perfect album title and a perfect album. Ellison died in ’96 at 29. The argument was always going to outlast the people making it.
